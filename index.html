Montgomery County Traffic Violations

Data Analysis Project

This was a final project completed alongside one other student in an Natural Computation course at UC San Diego. Building off our knowledge of another data analysis class taken in parallel, my partner and I were required to explore a topic of our choice and subsequently expand our understanding of Unsupervised Learning by choosing a real world data set and applying a technique we learned to questions we were interested in analyzing.
Since I was the primary coder and analyst in the group, any dataset we chose and questions we wanted to ask had to be manageable as we had limited analysis knowledge in addition to a short time restriction.

Background and Motivation

If you could easily visualize all of the traffic violations issued in the county you live in, what would you want to know?
Personally, I would want to know where most drivers are ticketed in my county so as to avoid this area while driving or to remember to be extra safe to avoid getting myself into trouble. As a person who has to make at least a 15-minute commute to the university on a daily basis, one of my fears is to be pulled over and issued a traffic ticket.
Having heard about the stereotypes about how officers have to meet their quota, especially nearing the end of the month makes me wonder if there may be some patterns among the traffic violations issued after all.

Dataset Description

With nearly 150,000 traffic violations recorded, this dataset contains detailed information including date, time, location, details, etc.

Questions

1) Are the traffic violations issued in Montgomery County regionalized based on different times throughout the day?
2) Do speeding traffic violations group out in different locations based on different times of day?

Analysis

Technique

We used K-Means Clustering since our questions had no right or wrong groupings among region or time. For this algorithm, we start by initializing our k means randomly. We then label the points to be in the cluster with the nearest mean, so each mean gets all of the points close to it in its set. Next we update the means, which is just the average of all points in the cluster. Finally, we stop when there is no longer a change in means or we meet our end condition.

Question 1

The results for our problem fell short of our expectations because the groupings of traffic incidents among time and location did not cluster out the way that we had hoped. Instead, no matter what k-value I used, each cluster seemed to be a pretty even spread of all locations throughout Montgomery County.

Question 2

After working on the first question, we learned that k-means is largely affected by the variance of the inputs- any input that has a huge variance compared to the other inputs would affect the way the clusters group out. Therefore, in order to run the clustering algorithm for our second question, I had to reprocess the data so all variances were similar.

Surprisingly, this worked really well and we got clusters that answered our original question. Furthermore, even after I ran the code multiple times, the clusters with the same k-values resulted in the same plot. From this, we interpretted that there does exist locations at certain times of day where speeding traffic violations are given out more than usual.
The mean locations we got from this show us the estimate of the time and places a driver should try to avoid in their commute if they are trying to avoid getting a speeding traffic violations, as these locations indicate where most speed traffic violations exist based on the past data.

Discussion

Overall, I learned a lot working on this project, from the logistics of the analysis techniques to just general project management skills. If we had more time, we would have extended our study to individual traffic violations (other than just speeding) to see if they group out in different locations based on different times of the day. With this information, we could try to predict what traffic violations occur based on location and time information.

Mobile Playlist Management

UI/UX Design

This mobile playlist application was created as a final project along with two others in a Usability and Information Architecture course at UC San Diego. Through a 3-week process, my team and I conducted user research, comparisons of good and less good user experience/workflow on various mobile music management apps, competetive analysis of said apps, and finally a wireframe redesign based on things we learned.

User Research

To better understand the context in which our system would have been used, I interviewed 3 people who regularly listen to music through their mobile phones. Through our combined interviews, my group and I created a list of 10 distinct user scenarios of using a mobile playlist app.

Comparisons of User Experience/Workflow

For this part of our project, we had to find screen sequences of other music apps that were worth imitating and ones to avoid in our redesign. For example, given the user scenario for creating a playlist from the playlist page, we found that Spotify had the better flow compared to SoundCloud.

Competetive Analysis

Here, we made a analysis of the navication structures and labels used in three different music applications: Spotify, SoundCloud, and Google Play Music. For each of our chosen music apps, we created a hierarchical sitemap with key page details showing what the main pages on the site are. Then, for each app, we compared the genre lists both within itself and between different apps.

Wireframe Redesign

Finally, we took what we learned about good practices in information architecture and functionality and applied it to the design of our own music app. We used Adobe Photoshop to create the high resolution wireframes, then imported the wireframes to Invision to be able prototype our design.

Mint

Website Design

Web application designed to provide users a visualization displaying infographics of data relating to San Diego demographics from the DELPHI database.

We aimed to create this project mainly for users who are in a stage of their lives in which they are deciding whether or not a college education is worth their time and money. The main end-goal of this project was to be able to provide users with a tool in which they could compare various analytical data pertaining to income and level of education. By aggregating data about poverty levels, education levels, median household income levels, and employment status, we hoped to be able to provide the user with visualizations in which they could analyze and make sense for themselves should there be any correlation that would show. In turn, we anticipate that this would help them answer their initial question thatbrought them to our page in the first place.

A secondary end-goal for this project was to be able to create a user-friendly website that complied with basic HCI principles while also incorporating various tools that improved the aesthetics and flow and added more meaning to our visualizations. We intended to design and implement a user-interface that integrated D3 graphics to allow us to dynamically display data so that our users would be able to interact with our website rather than just viewing static information.

This app was built with Leaflet, MapBox, D3, Node JS, Express, JQuery, and Bootstrap.
